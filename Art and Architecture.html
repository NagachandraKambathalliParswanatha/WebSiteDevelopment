Classic European art and architectural influences meet with an urbane, design-heavy aesthetic in Montréal and Québec City Hundreds of years of history meets new architectural innovation in Montréal, where a stroll down 18th-century cobblestone streets feels like time travel. Jump into the Expo 67 era at Olympic Stadium, Habitat 67 and Buckminster Fuller's Biosphere, and visit the Canadian Centre for Architecture's extensive local and international exhibitions. For its creative views on architecture, Montréal was appointed the first UNESCO City of Design in North American city Montréal’s jewel of urban design, the Quartier International, an eclectic mix of striking skyscrapers nestled alongside heritage buildings.

Preserving the Old montréal

Narrow cobbled streets, quaint shop fronts, ancient churches, centuries old structures, aged pavilions, warehouses and factories line the streets of Old Montreal and the adjacent Downtown and Old Port. One of the most beautiful Victorian business districts in North America. Everywhere, graceful grey stone façades attract your gaze, as did the warehouse-stores with customers in days gone by. These warehouse-salesrooms, dating from the 1850s to the 1880s, were used to receive, store and showcase both imported and domestic products.

Inuit Art and Aboriginal Art

The Inuit Art collection, which takes up the entire fourth level of the Claire and Marc Bourgie Pavilion of Quebec and Canadian Art, bears witness to the Inuit artistic practice, past and present. It illustrates how the Inuit identity draws on both ancient legends and modern-day changes to their way of life. early and contemporary Aboriginal Art is showcased in the Founding Identities gallery, highlighting the critical and introspective perspectives of the First Nations regarding their contact with Europeans. With over one million visitors a year, the Montreal Museum of fine arts is one of the most popular museums in the country. it is renowned for its encuclopedic collection featuring over 41000 workd of art the only one of it in canada and its original temporary exhibitions Explore its four pavilion, including the Claire and Marc Bourgie Pavilion of Quebec and canadian Art, Which showcases over one hundred Inuit and Aborginal works of art

MADONNA DELLA DEFESA CHURCH

MADONNA DELLA DEFESA CHURCH If you look up at the cupola behind the main altar of Little Italy's most famous church, you'll spot Montréal's most infamous piece of ecclesiastical portraiture The Madonna della Defesa (Our Lady of Protection) church was built in 1919 by the architect R. Montbirant based on the drawings of the artist Guido Nincheri Born in Florence, this Montréal artist also painted the church’s remarkable frescoes. It is Romanesque in style and laid out in a Greek-cross floorplan It was built by Italian immigrants to the city, specifically those from Molise, to commemorate the apparition of the Madonna in La Difesa, in Casacalenda, Molise The altar and balustrade date from 1950 and are made of Carrara marble. Next to the church, Dante Park celebrates the illustrious 14th-century poet and is a favourite meeting place for bocce players and passersby.

Sherbrooke Street

SherStreet, is a major east-west artery and at 31.3 kilometres (19.4 mi) in length, is the second longest street on the Island of Montreal. The street begins in the town of Montreal West and ends on the extreme tip of the island in Pointe-aux-Trembles. West of downtown, a mainly English-speaking clientele flocks to Sherbrooke Street for traditional grocery shopping, entertainment and clothing. The Victorian storefronts lend a British ambiance to the neighbourhood’s restaurants, pubs and shops. Once an enclave for Montréal’s wealthiest families, this posh area of the city is now lined with designer boutiques, antique stores, art galleries and many other places to pick up unique and high-end trends. Many of Québec’s leading fashion designers have set up shop on both de la Montagne and Crescent Streets nearby.
